         DYSGRAPHIA

dysgraphia is specific learning difficulty that affects written expression which impairs handwriting and fine motor skills. A child with dysgraphia struggles to write on straight lines and apply the appropriate spacing between words or may have letters scattered without the proper spacing. Often, some letters are omitted, especially vowels in the writing. The manner in which the wrist, body, or paper are positioned while writing usually look awkward. As a result, will tend to erase or cancel most of the time and these make the handwriting chaotic or difficult to read.


Writing is a complex process of generating letters and words (patterns and ideas) of a language in an expected format. It requires the coordination of thinking, processing the thought, Transferring the thought to the hand and producing meaningfully ideas in form of written expression. Inability to complete the sequence becomes a problem. Also, difficulty controlling fine motor skills, sequencing the hand movements, or controlling spatial accuracy can cause slow writing and non-language based dysgraphia.


Children with dysgraphia maybe bright with good verbal skills, vast vocabularies, oral fluency, wonderful ideas but they are unable to transfer those ideas to the book because they find writing laborious, however, parents and teachers expect them to write at the same speed as they speak and when they do not, they may be mistakenly thought of as lacking motivation or being careless.


Dysgraphia can occur alone or with other learning disabilities and disorders. Reconciling the needed support such as using alternative methods rather than writing goes a long way to help such children. Examples are reducing written workload, providing handouts, conducting assessment with technological based devices etc. In this case, the motor skills demand is put aside as long as knowledge is attained.
